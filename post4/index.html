What Does ‚ÄúVoice‚Äù or ‚ÄúStyle‚Äù Mean in Writing?

  • Word choice (formal vs casual, vocabulary level)
  • Sentence structure & rhythm (short vs long sentences, complexity)
  • Tone: humor, sarcasm, warmth, directness, distance, etc.
  • Use of punctuation, formatting, quirks (ellipses, dashes, capitalization)
  • Use of metaphors, examples, stories

AI models have the capability to approximate these features if you give enough cues. Large language models encode semantic relationships by placing words and phrases in high-dimensional spaces where meaning is represented by distance and similarity, and users can shift those relationships toward their own natural vocabulary by supplying sample text that the model uses as contextual guidance.

Why ‚Äúvoice borrowing‚Äù works (and what research says)

Models imitate style best when you show (examples) and tell (explicit descriptors). Industry docs consistently recommend example-based prompting and multi-shot examples; community reports echo that breaking style into traits (sentence length, punctuation habits, rhetorical moves) helps the model latch onto the right signals. How to Borrow a Voice: Step-by-Step Prompt Techniques

Technique A: Example Block + ‚ÄúAnalyze & Replicate‚Äù Prompt

  1. Collect 2-4 pieces of your own writing that you feel represent your voice. These might be emails, blog posts, internal docs. Prefer varied contexts if possible (some formal, some casual).
  2. Prompt to analyze style, ignoring content. Example prompt:
    Prompt Part 1 (style extraction):
Here are several excerpts from my writing:
Excerpt #1: ‚ÄúI‚Äôve noticed that when you try to boil the ocean, the small wins get lost, but focusing on narrow improvements compounds quickly.‚Äù
Excerpt #2: ‚ÄúOn Tuesday‚Äôs call, I felt the delivery could have been more concise; bullet points tend to help when people are tired.‚Äù
(add more)
Instruction: Based only on those excerpts, describe in detail my writing style: tone, vocabulary, sentence structure, rhythm, punctuation habits, use of metaphors or examples, formality level, etc. Don‚Äôt mention the topics. I want you to produce a ‚Äústyle profile‚Äù.
  3. Prompt to generate new text in that style:
    Using the style profile you generated, write a new passage (e.g. a short blog intro, or an email, or whatever your use case is) on [some topic]. Ensure it matches the style profile: vocabulary, tone, sentence length, punctuation habits, etc.
  4. Refinement loop: You compare the output to your own writing. Give feedback like: ‚Äútoo formal/too many long sentences / lacks humor / not enough casual phrasing,‚Äù and re-prompt.

Technique B: Agent / Persona Prompt

You can build a reusable persona or ‚Äúagent‚Äù prompt that sits at the top of everything you send. Example structure:

Persona definition (once):
You are ‚ÄúMyStyleAI,‚Äù a writing agent that writes as if it were me. My writing style has these features:
‚Äî I use conversational but professional tone, with occasional informal contractions (‚ÄúI‚Äôll,‚Äù ‚Äúdon‚Äôt‚Äù)
‚Äî I prefer short-to-moderate sentences; when needed, I sometimes include longer complex sentences but not too many
‚Äî I use analogies/metaphors often to explain technical concepts (e.g. ‚Äúlike boiling the ocean,‚Äù ‚Äúfiltering noise‚Äù)
‚Äî I include occasional mild humour or rhetorical questions (‚ÄúWhat‚Äôs the point if we can‚Äôt measure?‚Äù)
‚Äî I tend to avoid overly technical jargon unless audience is technical; when I do, I define it
‚Äî I often begin paragraphs with a hook sentence; I tend not to use bullet lists unless summarizing steps
‚Äî Punctuation style: minimal commas where possible, avoid overuse of ellipses, but I do use em-dashes (‚Äî) to set off clauses loosely.
Then for each prompt, precede with something like:
Prompt:
MyStyleAI: Write [type of content] about [topic] for [audience]. Make sure it follows the persona definition above.
You can store that persona block and reuse it.

Including Minor Typos / Idiosyncrasies

Over-polished AI text can feel synthetic. One way to make AI output feel more ‚Äúreal‚Äù or less synthetic is to include minor typos or quirks in the sample text to make the voice feel more human. Empirical work (and practical red teaming) shows many AI-text detectors are fragile; small edits and errors degrade their accuracy, with documented cases where deliberate typos/grammar perturbations materially reduce detection reliability. That doesn‚Äôt mean you should sabotage your writing ‚Äî it means that a few natural quirks can help preserve authenticity and avoid false positives. Examples of quirks:

  • Missing symbols or punctuation occasionally (e.g. missing comma, missing period, dash used instead of comma)
  • Slight inconsistencies (short vs long sentences)
  • Minor colloquialisms, contractions, filler words (‚Äújust‚Äù, ‚Äúyou know‚Äù, ‚Äúkind of‚Äù)
  • Deliberate non-standard style choices (e.g. beginning some lines with ‚ÄúAnd‚Äù, or using informal interjections)

When you include these in your exemplar text, and instruct the AI not to over-correct them (or even preserve them), the output tends to feel less ‚Äúsynthetic‚Äù and more like you / someone real. But use this carefully: too many errors look unprofessional; balance is key.

Benefits

  • Consistency across content: blog posts, emails, reports, etc. all ‚Äúsound like you.‚Äù
  • Efficiency: Once the style is encoded (via examples, persona prompt), you spend less time editing.
  • Authenticity: The quirks make writing feel more human, engaging.
  • Branding: For recruiters & industry pros, clear voice helps you stand out.

What not to do (and why your prompts flop)

  • Pure adjectives (‚Äúmake it punchy‚Äù) with no samples ‚Üí the model will default to generic ‚Äúinternet voice‚Äù.
  • Wall of context with no explicit instruction placement ‚Üí instructions fade. Put them top/bottom. OpenAI Cookbook
  • Abstractive style summaries only ‚Üí you‚Äôll lose cadence and punctuation signals. Prefer quotes for compression. arXiv

Ethics, permissions, and the ‚Äúdon‚Äôt be weird‚Äù clause

  • Always respect authorship and copyright: if you‚Äôre cloning someone else‚Äôs voice, ensure you have rights or permission.
  • Attribution: Be transparent when needed. In many contexts (e.g. journalism, professional writing) readers may expect disclosure if AI has been involved.
  • Bias & stereotypes: Style often encodes implicit bias. Be aware of what traits your exemplar text includes (e.g. gendered language, cultural idioms) and whether they are appropriate.
  • Over-reliance risk: if you always ask AI to clone, you might reduce your own voice development. Use it as tool, not crutch.

My recommendation when cloning your voice, your brand‚Äôs voice, or a voice is to have the right to use it. If you‚Äôre inspired by a public figure, keep it inspired-by rather than identifiable imitation in professional contexts. Disclose AI assistance where your org requires it. (Also: style often encodes cultural/identity cues; be mindful.)

Language models learn statistical signals ‚Äî not your soul. When you provide concrete exemplars and explicit trait labels, you‚Äôre reducing ambiguity and increasing the chance the model locks onto the right distributional cues (cadence, punctuation, lexical register). That‚Äôs why examples + trait bullets beat adjectives ‚Äî and why where you place instructions matters in long prompts. Prompt engineering for Claude's long context window ‚Äì Anthropic

Conclusion & Your 20-minute setup checklist

  1. Gather 2‚Äì3 authentic samples (250‚Äì400 words total) of your own writing that you feel ‚Äúsounds like you".
  2. Build your prompt including: style sample, clear voice/tone descriptors, target audience, structure.
  3. Build Exercise 1 and Skeleton 1 into your notebook or agent, then test your prompts
  4. Refine prompt or give feedback to agent (e.g. ‚Äúthis is too formal,‚Äù ‚Äúuse more contractions,‚Äù etc.).
  5. If too polished, use small imperfections consciously (typos / quirks), or if you want more human feel.
  6. Adjust prompt length to your model‚Äôs limits, making sure exemplar + instructions + target content stay within context window.
